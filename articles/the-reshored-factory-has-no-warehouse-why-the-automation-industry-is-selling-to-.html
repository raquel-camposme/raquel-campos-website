The Reshored Factory Has No Warehouse: Why the Automation Industry Is Selling to the Wrong Buyer

US manufacturing is growing at its fastest rate since 2021 while cutting jobs — and the automation industry is celebrating the wrong part of that signal. The buyer who needs them most doesn't know it yet.

reshoringmanufacturing automationintralogisticswarehouse designsupply chain strategy

There is a category of problem that only becomes visible in hindsight, when the people who could have acted on it are already paying the consequences. The reshored American factory is one of those problems — not because reshoring is failing, but because it's succeeding in a way that has quietly outpaced the industry built to support it.

Here is the specific signal worth paying attention to this week: US manufacturing output is growing at its fastest rate since 2021, and at the same time, manufacturing employment is falling [16]. S&P Global described it as "an ongoing bifurcation of the economy." That framing is accurate but insufficient. What's actually happening is that the reshoring wave — the one the Treasury Secretary is now publicly calling a structural imperative for US supply chains [43] — is arriving pre-automated. Factories are coming back. Workers are not coming with them. And the intralogistics industry, which should be perfectly positioned to serve this moment, is almost entirely pointed in the wrong direction.

The Factory Floor Is the Easy Part

When a manufacturer reshores a production line, the technical challenge that gets the most attention and budget is the production process itself. Equipment procurement, process validation, workforce training on automated systems — these are well-understood problems with established vendors, project management methodologies, and internal champions who've done it before. The factory floor is complex, but it's a known category of complexity.

What doesn't get the same attention — and frequently gets spec'd late, by teams who have never run a modern fulfillment or distribution operation — is internal material flow. How does raw material move from receiving dock to line-side buffer? How does work-in-progress move between production cells? How does finished goods inventory get staged, sequenced, and released to outbound? These questions sit at the intersection of manufacturing operations and intralogistics, and they don't belong cleanly to either discipline.

The physical infrastructure to support reshored manufacturing is being built right now. CSX just completed a $495 million expansion of the Howard Street Tunnel in Baltimore, enabling full double-stacked rail clearance at the Port of Baltimore [17]. Rail intermodal volumes are already up 12.1% year over year [51]. The freight infrastructure is being stood up in anticipation of domestic manufacturing volume. But the internal material handling systems inside those factories — the conveyors, AMRs, goods-to-person zones, buffer management systems, and WMS configurations — are being treated as an afterthought in most greenfield industrial builds.

This is not a minor implementation detail. It is where throughput is won or lost. An automated production line fed by manual cart pushes and tribal knowledge about where the WIP buffer lives is not a competitive asset — it's a liability with a modern exterior.

The Buyer the Industry Doesn't Know How To Talk To

The automation and intralogistics industry has spent the last decade developing its sales motion, its case study library, and its ROI framework around one primary buyer: the distribution center operator. E-commerce fulfillment, retail replenishment, omnichannel DC retrofit — this is the terrain where AS/RS systems, goods-to-person technology, and warehouse orchestration platforms have been validated and sold. The language of the industry is the language of picks per hour, order cycle time, and cost-per-unit-shipped.

The newly reshored manufacturer doesn't speak that language. Their internal vocabulary is units per shift, OEE, line balance, and takt time. They think in production rhythms, not fulfillment waves. Their material flow problems manifest as line stoppages, excess WIP accumulation, and expedited freight costs when staging buffers are wrong — not as mis-picks or late shipments. The symptom set is different enough that the standard automation pitch doesn't land, and the standard discovery process doesn't surface the right pain.

There's a deeper issue underneath the language mismatch: the reshored manufacturer typically has no internal intralogistics competency. Their operations team knows how to run a factory. Their procurement team knows how to buy production equipment. Nobody in the building has ever designed a receiving-to-line-side material flow, selected a WMS for a manufacturing environment, or implemented an AMR fleet for WIP transport. They don't know what they don't know, which means they're not issuing RFPs for intralogistics solutions — they're discovering the problem six months after go-live when throughput is 30% below target and the root cause is a buffer sizing error that any experienced integrator would have caught in the design phase.

This is precisely the dynamic that the supply chain tech ROI literature keeps identifying without fully diagnosing. Organizations consistently miss technology ROI targets because they prioritize software selection over strategy, data governance, and business alignment [52]. In the reshored manufacturing context, the problem appears even earlier: the buyer doesn't know they need the technology, can't articulate what decision they need it to support, and has no internal champion with the vocabulary to evaluate it. The result isn't bad implementation — it's no conversation at all.

What a Repositioned Go-to-Market Would Actually Look Like

The orchestration platforms and automation vendors who figure out how to lead with manufacturing intralogistics — not DC fulfillment — have an opportunity to own a category that is being created in real time by macroeconomic forces entirely outside their control.

That repositioning requires three specific things that are largely absent from current go-to-market strategies.

First, a diagnostic offer, not a product pitch. The reshored manufacturer needs someone to walk their production floor and translate what they see into intralogistics requirements. Most vendors are configured to respond to RFPs, not to create them. The integrators who build a capability to conduct manufacturing intralogistics assessments — and who give that capability away as a loss leader — will create deal flow that their competitors can't access because their competitors are waiting to be invited.

Second, ROI language anchored in production metrics, not fulfillment metrics. The value of a properly designed line-side buffer management system is measured in OEE improvement and production hours recovered from material starvation events. If the business case is presented in picks-per-hour and cost-per-unit, it will fail to connect. The automation industry needs a manufacturing-native financial model, and it largely doesn't have one yet.

Third, an honest engagement with the design gap. The intralogistics solution for a reshored manufacturer is not a product — it's an architecture that spans receiving, storage, line-side replenishment, WIP handling, and finished goods staging. Nobody on the manufacturer's team is holding that architecture together. The vendor or integrator who positions themselves as the architect, rather than the component supplier, will win disproportionately in this market.

The signal this week is unambiguous. Manufacturing is accelerating [16], physical freight infrastructure is being built [17], and government policy is actively pushing for supply chain restructuring [43]. The reshored factory is arriving. It will need intralogistics. And right now, the industry that should be meeting it is largely still talking to the DC operations manager who already knows what an AMR is.

The decision that matters today is not which technology to sell. It's which buyer to find.
