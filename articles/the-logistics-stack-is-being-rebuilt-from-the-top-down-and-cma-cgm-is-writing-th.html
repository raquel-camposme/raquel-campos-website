The Logistics Stack Is Being Rebuilt From the Top Down, and CMA CGM Is Writing the Blueprint

CMA CGM just bought FedEx's contract logistics unit for $1.4 billion. That's not a port operator diversifying. That's a company assembling a vertically integrated logistics stack — and it's further along than most people realize.

What $1.4 Billion Actually Buys

When CMA CGM agreed to acquire FedEx Supply Chain this week [1], the coverage landed mostly in the maritime and logistics trade press as a straightforward deal story. French shipping giant buys U.S. contract logistics business. CEVA grows in North America. FedEx sharpens focus on healthcare and aerospace. Boxes checked.

But I keep watching these CMA CGM moves because they don't fit the usual pattern of a shipping line dabbling in logistics. This is something more deliberate, and the FedEx Supply Chain acquisition is probably the clearest signal yet of what they're actually building.

The deal nearly triples CEVA's North American contract logistics footprint overnight [1]. Combined, the two operations will run around 150 warehouses and more than 240 locations across North America, with approximately 20,000 staff. That's not a bolt-on. That's a platform. And attached to the acquisition comes a multi-year agreement where CMA CGM becomes a preferred ocean carrier for FedEx, plus a separate arrangement on air cargo capacity.

So in one transaction, CMA CGM got warehouse square footage, fulfillment capability, a commercial pipeline for its core ocean business, and a foothold in air freight. At $1.4 billion, that is an extraordinary amount of supply chain surface area per dollar spent.

The Stack They're Building

To understand why this matters, you have to look at the full sequence.

CMA CGM acquired CEVA Logistics in 2019. Then Bolloré Logistics in 2024 for $5.2 billion, one of the largest logistics acquisitions in the company's history [1]. Along the way, CEVA absorbed Ingram Micro's CLS division and GEFCO. Now FedEx Supply Chain. Each acquisition adds a different capability layer: freight management, contract logistics, fulfillment, air capacity.

What CMA CGM is assembling is a vertically integrated logistics stack. Ocean, air, warehousing, fulfillment, last mile. All under one commercial roof, with CEVA as the integration layer and CMA CGM's vessel network as the foundation.

This is not new as a concept. Amazon built something similar, starting from demand and working backwards into logistics infrastructure. Maersk has been attempting a parallel transformation for years, with mixed results. But CMA CGM is doing it faster, and with a more pragmatic approach. Rather than building organically, they're acquiring mature operations with existing customer bases and physical infrastructure, then wiring them together.

The question worth asking is: what does this feel like from the other side of the contract?

The View From the Shipper

If you are a mid-size manufacturer or retailer managing an international supply chain right now, the consolidation happening at the top of the logistics market deserves attention.

For years, the model was to unbundle. Hire a freight forwarder here, a 3PL there, a separate last-mile provider, maybe a customs broker, and orchestrate them yourself or through a TMS. The complexity was annoying but the optionality was valuable. You could switch providers. You could negotiate on individual legs. You could avoid lock-in.

That model still works. But it is becoming harder to execute well as the major players integrate vertically. When CMA CGM can offer you a commercial rate on ocean freight contingent on also using CEVA for warehousing and fulfillment, the economics of staying unbundled start to look different. Not necessarily worse, but different. The negotiating dynamics shift.

C.H. Robinson's acquisition of DeSpir Logistics this week [2] tells a parallel story at a different layer of the market. DeSpir specializes in secure transportation for high-value, mission-critical freight. That's a niche capability, but it feeds directly into C.H. Robinson's broader push to cover more of the logistics complexity that sophisticated shippers face. Freight brokers are also integrating up and down the stack, just more incrementally.

The industry is consolidating around full-service providers who can handle more of the chain. That trajectory has been visible for a few years. What is accelerating it now is a combination of shipper demand for resilience (after 2020-2023 taught everyone what single-point-of-failure looks like) and rising truck freight rates, which were up 31% in spot markets year-over-year through May [3], making the cost of a fragmented, uncoordinated network more visible and painful.

Where Onshoring Fits

There is a second thread running through this week's news that connects, though not obviously at first.

Lovesac is moving production of its Sactionals line to U.S. manufacturing this summer [4], eliminating overseas production for its primary revenue driver. The company is explicit about the reason: tariff exposure, freight cycle risk, and a desire for faster fulfillment. The factory won't be owned or operated by Lovesac, which is the smart move. Contract manufacturing domestically, rather than building owned capacity, keeps flexibility while capturing the logistics and tariff benefits.

But here's what I find interesting about the Lovesac story as a signal. This is a direct-to-consumer furniture brand, not an industrial manufacturer with decades of supply chain infrastructure. If a mid-size DTC retailer can execute a full manufacturing geography shift in response to tariff pressure, the structural barriers to onshoring for soft goods and consumer products are lower than the conventional wisdom suggests.

The catch, and Lovesac's CEO noted this directly, is that the U.S.-made product will be different from the Vietnam-made version. Manufacturing differences mean the product isn't identical. That is a meaningful trade-off, and it matters for brand positioning and customer expectations. But the company seems to have decided that supply chain resilience and tariff insulation are worth the product variation. That's a real strategic choice, not just a PR move.

The implication for logistics providers, including the CMA CGM/CEVA stack being assembled, is that the reshoring and nearshoring wave genuinely changes the geographic distribution of freight flows. More domestic manufacturing means more domestic warehousing requirements, more inbound raw material flows from different origin points, and potentially shorter but more complex last-mile fulfillment patterns. A logistics integrator with deep North American contract logistics capacity is better positioned for that shift than one whose infrastructure is oriented primarily around international freight.

FedEx Supply Chain, with its North American warehouse and distribution platform, is exactly the kind of asset that becomes more valuable as manufacturing moves closer to the consumer. I don't think that timing is coincidental.

The Hormuz Variable

One thing I want to flag that is not getting enough attention in the logistics consolidation narrative: the Strait of Hormuz situation is not fully resolved.

The U.S.-Iran ceasefire MoU reduced acute risk, but logistics normalization is expected to take months [5], and VLCC market data from Q2 tells a more complicated story. The Hormuz closure effectively forced the VLCC market to rebuild around Fujairah and the Gulf of Oman as alternative loading points [6]. The Baltic Exchange introduced a new benchmark route tracking Mina al-Fahal to Ningbo cargoes specifically because the old benchmarks no longer reflected actual market conditions.

Energy price pressure had been a significant inflation driver, and the ceasefire eased that somewhat. But if you are a logistics operator or shipper whose network touches Middle Eastern origin points, the past quarter should have demonstrated clearly that the physical routing of global crude flows can be disrupted faster than any contingency plan accounts for.

For CMA CGM specifically, this is relevant. A more vertically integrated logistics player is also more exposed to cascading disruption. When you own the ocean leg, the warehousing, and the last mile, a disruption on one leg is harder to absorb because it runs through more of your own infrastructure rather than being distributed across multiple independent providers. The flexibility trade-off of vertical integration is real, and I don't think it gets discussed enough in the coverage of these deals.

What I'm Actually Watching

The CMA CGM/FedEx Supply Chain deal is the biggest structural signal of the week, but I think the more important question it raises is about the next few years of shipper strategy.

We are entering a period where the top five or six logistics providers will have the capability to service most of a shipper's supply chain across most geographies. That is genuinely new. The commercial implications for shippers who have built their operations around a fragmented, multi-provider model are significant.

I'm not convinced vertically integrated logistics is always better for the shipper. The cost structures are often less transparent. The switching costs are higher. And there is a real question about whether a single provider with exposure across every leg of your chain increases or decreases systemic risk.

But I do think the companies that understand what CMA CGM is actually building, not just another acquisition, but a bet on where supply chain structure is going, will be better positioned to negotiate with them, decide whether to use them, and think clearly about the alternatives while those alternatives still exist.

The stack is being rebuilt. That is the story worth following.
