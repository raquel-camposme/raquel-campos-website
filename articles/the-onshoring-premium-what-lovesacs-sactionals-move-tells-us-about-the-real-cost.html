The Onshoring Premium: What Lovesac's Sactionals Move Tells Us About the Real Cost of Tariff Strategy

Lovesac is moving production of its core sofa line to the US this summer. The product will be different from the version made in Vietnam. That detail is more important than the announcement itself.

onshoringtariffsconsumer goodsmanufacturingsupply chain resilience

The product changes when the factory moves

Lovesac made an interesting admission on its June 11 earnings call. The company is on track to begin domestic manufacturing of its Sactionals line this summer, CEO Shawn Nelson confirmed, with a ramp-up plan that would eventually eliminate overseas production for its primary revenue driver entirely [1]. That part of the story got the headlines.

But buried in the same call was something worth sitting with. The US-made Sactionals product will differ from the version made in Vietnam or elsewhere in Asia. Due to manufacturing differences, full feature parity isn't being achieved, at least not initially.

That's the real signal here. Not the announcement of onshoring, which was telegraphed months ago as part of a four-pronged tariff response strategy. The signal is that manufacturing geography shapes the product itself. When you move production, you don't just move costs and lead times. You move constraints, capabilities, and sometimes, what you're able to make.

This matters far beyond furniture.

What "tariff strategy" actually requires

Lovesac's situation is worth understanding in some detail because it's a cleaner version of what a lot of consumer goods companies are quietly working through right now.

The company had already been diversifying away from China before this latest move, shifting production to Vietnam as part of an earlier tariff response. Now, with broader tariff pressure continuing and no clear resolution in sight, they're taking the next step: contracting with a US factory (one they won't own or operate) to manufacture the core product domestically [1].

The expected benefits are the ones you'd expect from a textbook supply chain resilience pitch: reduced cost volatility, faster fulfillment, less dependency on long international freight cycles. President Mary Fox told investors the supply chain is "becoming more efficient, more resilient and better positioned to support our next phase of growth." That's the framing companies use when they've made a decision and need to land it cleanly with shareholders.

But underneath that framing is a real operational challenge. Domestic manufacturing in the US is not simply a faster version of Asian manufacturing. Labor costs are different. Process capabilities are different. Supplier ecosystems for materials and components are built differently. You can't always make the same product the same way in a different geography, especially if the original manufacturing base developed over years of optimization, supplier co-location, and process refinement.

The Lovesac case makes this explicit in a way that most companies avoid acknowledging publicly. The product is changing. They're also redesigning core inserts for the Sactionals line to align with the US production model [1]. That's not a small footnote. That's product engineering being driven by manufacturing geography rather than consumer insight. The supply chain is now influencing the roadmap.

The US manufacturing ecosystem question

I keep watching for cases where the reshoring narrative collides with the actual capacity constraints of US manufacturing, because I think that collision is going to define a lot of strategic decisions over the next few years.

There's a version of the onshoring story that gets told a lot right now: tariffs go up, cost equations shift, American factories become competitive again, supply chains come home. It's tidy. And it's partly true. Truck freight spot rates were up 31% year-over-year in May, which tells you domestic logistics is tightening as goods move differently [2], but that's a separate problem.

The part that gets underplayed is that the US manufacturing base for many consumer goods categories was allowed to thin considerably over the past three decades. You can't just flip a switch and reconstitute it. The specialized labor, the component suppliers, the process knowledge, the tooling, the factory infrastructure for specific goods categories, those things take time to rebuild. And in the interim, companies making the shift will face exactly what Lovesac is describing: they'll make a different version of the product than they were making before.

That's not necessarily fatal. Sometimes the US version is good enough. Sometimes it's better in ways that matter to domestic consumers. Faster replenishment, for instance, means Lovesac can respond more quickly to demand signals rather than sitting on inventory hedged against long ocean freight cycles. There are real advantages here, particularly for a direct-to-consumer brand that competes on fulfillment speed and product flexibility.

But companies that are treating onshoring as a pure cost and resilience play, without factoring in the product and process implications, are going to get surprised.

Who actually bears the risk in this model

One detail in the Lovesac announcement worth noting: the factory won't be owned or operated by Lovesac. They're contracting with an existing manufacturer. This is a sensible capital structure for a company that doesn't want to be in the factory ownership business, and it parallels how a lot of onshoring is actually happening, through contract manufacturers who are investing in new or expanded US capacity, not through brand owners building their own plants.

The economics of that model put a different kind of pressure on the supply chain relationship. When you own the factory, you control the investment decisions, the technology choices, the labor practices, the capacity expansion timing. When you contract with someone else's factory, you're dependent on their decisions about all of those things.

For Lovesac, this probably works fine. Sactionals are not precision-engineered components with sub-millimeter tolerances. Modular sofa systems have relatively forgiving manufacturing requirements, and the contract model gives them flexibility to scale without committing capital. The redesign of core inserts might actually be an opportunity to simplify the product architecture in ways that make US contract manufacturing easier and more consistent.

But I think the broader question of who owns manufacturing capacity in a reshoring-driven world is underexamined. If brands continue to contract rather than own, the manufacturing investment risk concentrates in a different set of players. Those contract manufacturers need to make long-horizon capital bets on factory equipment and labor capacity, betting that the tariff environment stays hostile enough to make the economics work, and that their brand customers won't pivot again if trade policy shifts.

That's a real risk. The USMCA is reportedly not being extended by the current US administration, which would start a decade-long countdown to the end of that trade pact [3]. Tariff refund policies are expanding [3]. The policy environment is moving fast, and not always in a consistent direction. Manufacturers making 10-year capital commitments need stability that the current political landscape isn't obviously providing.

What the semiconductor parallel tells us

The McKinsey piece this week on semiconductor supply mapping frames this well at a macro level [4]. As geopolitics shift, more countries are wooing semiconductor manufacturers to enhance resilience. Demand for advanced semiconductors continues to grow. But the actual process of relocating semiconductor manufacturing, as the CHIPS Act implementation has shown, is measured in years, not quarters, and it involves deep ecosystem-building, not just factory construction.

Consumer goods are simpler than semiconductors. But the underlying dynamic is the same. You can announce an onshoring initiative relatively quickly. You can sign a contract manufacturing deal, start redesigning products for local production capabilities, and tell shareholders the supply chain is becoming more resilient. The harder part is building the ecosystem depth that makes that manufacturing genuinely competitive over time, not just tariff-forced viable.

What Lovesac is doing is honest about this, which is actually refreshing. They're not pretending the US product will be identical. They're redesigning around the constraints. That's an adaptive strategy rather than a wishful one.

The broader supply chain implication

If this pattern holds across more categories, and I think it will, then the supply chain implications extend well beyond manufacturing decisions. Procurement teams sourcing materials for US factories need different supplier relationships than those sourcing for Asian contract manufacturers. Inbound logistics flows shift. Inventory management changes when lead times compress from 60-plus days ocean transit to domestic delivery cycles. Demand forecasting can operate with shorter horizons. The whole operating model needs recalibration.

The companies that navigate this well won't be the ones that treat onshoring as a tariff hedge. They'll be the ones that redesign their supply chain operating model around the new geography, the way Lovesac is redesigning its product. They'll ask what becomes possible when the factory is domestic, not just what stays viable when it moves there.

Faster replenishment cycles could mean lower safety stock. Lower safety stock means less working capital tied up in inventory. That's a real structural advantage if you can execute it consistently. Combined with the product development agility that comes from being able to iterate physically close to the manufacturing site, there's a version of this story where US production genuinely improves the business, not just protects it from tariffs.

Whether Lovesac gets there will depend on the execution. But the strategic direction is coherent. The signal worth watching is which other consumer goods categories start seeing the same product-meets-manufacturing-capability conversation play out publicly. That's when we'll know reshoring has moved from political narrative to operational reality.
