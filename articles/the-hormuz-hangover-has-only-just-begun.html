The Hormuz Hangover Has Only Just Begun

Container rates are near $9,000 on the East Coast lane, tanker transits through the Strait of Hormuz have plunged, and a ceasefire that held for weeks just collapsed. Most companies are still treating this as a temporary spike. I don't think it is.

hormuzocean freighttariffssupply chain disruptiongeopolitics

There's a version of this story that reads as a familiar cycle. Geopolitical tension flares in the Middle East, freight rates spike, shippers scramble, the situation eventually stabilizes, and everything goes back to something like normal. That version of events has been approximately true enough, often enough, that it's become the default mental model for how to handle the Strait of Hormuz.

I think that model is wrong this time, and I want to explain why.

When President Trump declared the ceasefire "over" while meeting NATO leaders in Turkey last week, it wasn't just a diplomatic headline. It was the signal that the market had been hoping wouldn't come. Container rates on the Asia-East Coast US lane were already approaching $9,000 per FEU, up 8% week-over-week, according to FreightWaves [1], even before that announcement. The surge was already happening, driven by general rate increases and peak season surcharges that carriers had locked in from July 1. The war restarting added volatility to a freight market that had no slack to absorb it.

And then there's Hormuz.

What the tanker numbers are actually telling us

Tanker transit through the Strait of Hormuz has plunged as the ceasefire has fractured, according to Supply Chain Dive [2]. That's not just an energy story. The Strait carries roughly 20-21% of global oil traded by sea. When tanker volumes fall there, it lands in energy prices, then in fuel surcharges, then in every mode of freight simultaneously. It's one of the few chokepoints on earth with the ability to hit ocean, air, and truck simultaneously, because they all run on energy.

Logistics Management noted last week that while the Strait had briefly reopened, supply chains face a long road to recovery [3]. And that was written before the ceasefire collapsed. We're now back inside the disruption, not recovering from it.

The air freight market is already showing what that pressure looks like. Air cargo spot rates were up 38% year-over-year in June, driven largely by AI and semiconductor demand, according to Supply Chain Dive [4]. That's mostly a separate story, about chips and data infrastructure. But those two demand streams, tech-driven air cargo and crisis-driven modal shift from ocean, compete for the same physical capacity. Add them together and you get a cargo market where shippers who need to move fast will pay a lot more than they expect.

The tariff layer makes it worse

Here's what I keep watching for in situations like this: the moments when multiple independent pressures arrive at the same time. Right now there are at least three.

First, the Hormuz disruption is constraining ocean freight capacity and pushing rates up on multiple lanes, not just those touching the Gulf. When carriers reroute, schedules slip, and the ripple effect on port congestion is real. The Loadstar noted this week that Indian Ocean and Europe trade is already experiencing congestion and schedule reliability problems [5], which tracks with what you'd expect from a Hormuz disruption.

Second, US import volumes are on track to hit an all-time record in July. The National Retail Federation data released this week forecasts container import volumes above 2 million TEUs for the month [6], driven by retailers and importers front-loading ahead of an expected new round of tariffs on forced labor in 60 countries, likely starting in August. Temporary 10% Section 122 global tariffs expire July 24, but that relief is being read by most importers as a brief window before things get worse, not a genuine softening.

Third, Trump's decision to skip aircraft tariffs for now, while Ford, Nestlé, and others are actively seeking duty reprieves on other goods [7], is creating an uneven and unpredictable tariff landscape. Companies that thought they had a plan are now managing exemption applications alongside disrupted transit lanes alongside record import volumes. That's a lot of moving pieces for procurement and logistics teams that are already stretched.

When I look at those three things together, what I see is not a peak-season bump that will normalize in September. What I see is a market where structural uncertainty has become the baseline condition.

The freight recession is "over," but not in the way it matters

There's a mostly separate story running in parallel in US trucking that deserves to be read alongside this. DAT Freight & Analytics reported this week that dry van spot rates have topped contract rates for the first time since February 2022, and flatbed rates have hit a record high [8]. A Bloomberg Intelligence analyst, cited by FreightWaves [9], is positioning this as the end of the freight recession. Truckload and LTL stocks are up. The cycle is turning.

But DAT was careful to note something important: the rate recovery is being driven by tight capacity, not by stronger freight demand. These are very different things. When rates rise because capacity has left the market (carriers that went out of business during the three-year recession), the recovery is structurally fragile. It doesn't take much of a demand shock, in either direction, to knock it sideways.

Now put that fragile trucking recovery into a context where ocean imports are about to hit record highs, Hormuz is disrupting transoceanic lanes, and a new round of tariffs in August may shift demand patterns again. Drayage, transloading, inland distribution, warehouse intake, all of that connects to the ports. A record July import month doesn't stay at the port. It moves inland. Into already tight truck capacity. At already elevated rates.

I'm not predicting a collapse. But the probability of a very rough August and September for US domestic logistics has increased meaningfully this week, and I don't think it's priced into most companies' planning assumptions.

What this means for decisions being made right now

The companies in the clearest danger are those who have already committed to a tariff front-loading strategy (heavy import volumes through July) without accounting for how that inventory gets from port to shelf. The ocean booking was the easy part. The inland move is where the friction is building.

For manufacturers, the Hormuz disruption adds a specific risk to energy-intensive production, not just to inbound materials. If fuel prices spike and sustain, the cost base changes. Companies that haven't built fuel cost scenarios into their Q3 planning should do that now, not in September.

For retailers, there's a real inventory risk hiding inside the current surge. Front-loading is rational when you're trying to beat a tariff. It becomes a problem if the tariffs don't land exactly when expected, or if demand softens, and you're sitting on pre-bought inventory that was priced at $9,000-a-container freight rates. The NRF data showing record import volumes [6] is also, if you squint at it slightly, a potential inventory build that could correct hard in Q4.

For 3PLs and logistics operators, this is one of those moments where customers who thought they had capacity locked up will discover they don't. Especially on the drayage side. The gap between contract rates and spot rates closing, or inverting, is a signal that contract capacity commitments made during the freight recession may not fully hold.

The part nobody is building into their models

What concerns me most isn't the near-term disruption. Near-term disruption is visible and therefore manageable, at least partially. What concerns me is the medium-term structural shift in how companies think about Hormuz as a permanent risk variable rather than an occasional one.

The Loadstar's coverage this week flagged that carriers are actively reshaping their strategies around persistent port congestion and volatile ocean markets [5]. That's not crisis response language. That's industry restructuring language. When carriers start redesigning their network logic because of a disruption, the routes that emerge from that redesign don't just snap back. They become the new baseline.

For companies that source from the Gulf, or whose competitors do, this is a moment to think harder about where your exposure actually sits. Not just in terms of lanes or transit times, but in terms of the energy inputs that run your production, the raw materials that transit through that region, and the cost assumptions embedded in your supplier contracts.

The Strait of Hormuz has been a latent risk for decades. What's different now is the combination of an actively fractured ceasefire, a tariff environment that's compressing the margin for error on inventory timing, and a domestic freight market recovering on tight capacity rather than strong demand. Each of those factors would be manageable in isolation. Together, they reduce the buffer that supply chains depend on.

This is the kind of moment where the companies that come out ahead aren't necessarily the ones with the most sophisticated hedging strategies. They're the ones who looked at the signals early enough to have options.
