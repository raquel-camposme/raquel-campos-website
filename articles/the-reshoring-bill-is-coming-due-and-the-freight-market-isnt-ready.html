The Reshoring Bill Is Coming Due — and the Freight Market Isn't Ready

Micron is spending $250 billion to build chips in America. Toyota is moving Tacoma production to Texas. Apple and Broadcom are pledging billions more. Reshoring is no longer a policy aspiration — it's a construction project. But the freight market underpinning all of it is already running out of room.

I've been watching the reshoring wave for a couple of years now, mostly through the lens of policy and corporate announcements. This week something shifted for me. When you line up what's happening in semiconductor manufacturing, automotive production, electrical manufacturing, and the freight market simultaneously, a tension emerges that I don't think the industry is fully pricing in.

Let me try to explain what I mean.

A lot of stuff is being built, all at once

Micron announced this week that it's investing more than $250 billion in U.S. manufacturing through 2035, with construction already underway on what would be the largest semiconductor manufacturing site in U.S. history, in Clay, New York. According to Supply Chain Brain [1], the company has set a goal to produce 40% of its DRAM chips domestically, and it's putting $3 billion toward broader U.S. semiconductor supply chain development, including $500 million to help Taiwanese chipmaker GlobalWafers build a wafer facility in Sherman, Texas.

That's one announcement. Alongside it, Manufacturing Dive reported [2] that Apple and Broadcom have pledged $30 billion for U.S. chip production. And from the FreightWaves Borderlands column [3], Toyota is spending $3.6 billion to double its San Antonio plant and gradually move Tacoma production from Baja California, Mexico, a transition expected to take four years and add 2,000 jobs.

These are not small, symbolic gestures. These are decade-scale construction programs involving massive physical infrastructure: fabrication plants, wafer facilities, assembly lines, supplier clusters. Each one of them needs raw materials moving in, equipment installed, components flowing between facilities, and finished goods moving out. And that's before the supplier ecosystems that grow up around major manufacturing sites start generating their own freight.

U.S. electrical manufacturers are pushing in the same direction, though with more friction. A Supply Chain Brain feature published today [4] details how the industry is lobbying for support to expand domestic production while simultaneously absorbing 50% tariffs on imported steel and aluminum under Section 232. The squeeze is real: they want to build more in America, but the inputs they need to do that are more expensive because of tariffs on the same categories the administration says it wants to protect.

The freight market is already under strain

Here's where I get concerned. The Logistics Managers' Index, covered by FreightWaves this week [5], shows transportation capacity hitting near-record lows. Shippers are struggling to find capacity, and prices are moving in ways not seen since 2022. At the same time, the Inbound Ocean TEUs Index is holding near annual highs, driven by a wave of container bookings that FreightWaves analyst Zach Strickland attributes [6] to a combination of tariff-driven front-loading and what he describes as possibly the most "normal" demand environment since before COVID.

So the baseline freight market, before any of this reshoring manufacturing actually comes online, is already tight.

Trucking M&A is accelerating in parallel. FreightWaves reported [7] that private equity and strategic buyers are re-evaluating trucking assets as freight rates stabilize and earnings profiles improve, with analysts raising the possibility of a trucking M&A supercycle. That's an interesting signal on its own. When money starts consolidating around trucking assets, it usually means someone expects sustained pricing power, not a temporary blip.

I keep watching for the moment when the scale of reshoring investment starts colliding with the physical realities of domestic freight infrastructure, and I think we may be approaching it faster than the industry realizes.

The Toyota case is worth sitting with

The Tacoma move is a useful case study. Toyota isn't just shifting an assembly line, it's relocating an entire production system across a four-year window. FreightWaves reported [3] that the expansion adds roughly 2.5 million square feet to the San Antonio campus. Supplier networks that currently serve the Baja California plant will need to either establish U.S. presence, ship across the border at higher cost (Mexico is actively pressing to remove tariffs on autos and steel in the USMCA review, as Supply Chain Dive noted [8]), or be replaced by domestic alternatives.

That reconfiguration generates freight. A lot of it. New supplier relationships mean new transport lanes. Construction materials for the expanded facility move by truck and rail. Finished vehicles move out of San Antonio. The Tundra and Sequoia already move through that system; the Tacoma adds another layer.

And Toyota is one company. Multiply this across the semiconductor, electrical, and broader manufacturing commitments being announced right now, and the cumulative freight demand implication is substantial.

The electrical manufacturing problem is a preview

I think the situation facing U.S. electrical manufacturers, as described in the Supply Chain Brain feature [4], deserves more attention than it's getting as a signal. This industry wants to reshore. It's being encouraged to reshore by policy. But the Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum are raising the cost of domestic production inputs, and the industry's lobbying position, fewer tariffs on inputs, more investment incentives, isn't obviously compatible with the current administration's approach.

What this creates is a class of manufacturers caught between a policy directive to build at home and an import cost structure that makes building at home more expensive. They'll do it anyway, because they have to, because customers and contracts demand it. But the margins get thinner, the timelines get longer, and the freight cost environment they're building into is increasingly unfavorable.

This is the part of reshoring that doesn't show up in the announcements. The Micron press release doesn't mention that it will generate years of inbound heavy freight for construction equipment, specialized manufacturing tools (many of which still come from Japan, the Netherlands, and Germany), process chemicals, and raw silicon wafers. The Toyota announcement doesn't highlight that transitioning production over four years means running dual supply chains simultaneously for part of that period, which is expensive and logistically complex.

What the air cargo signal tells us

Supply Chain Dive's logistics newsletter flagged something else this week [9]: AI and chip demand is spurring air cargo growth. This connects directly. When you're trying to build advanced semiconductor manufacturing capacity in the United States, some of the most time-sensitive components and equipment don't move by ocean. They move by air, because the lead times in chip manufacturing are punishing, and the cost of a delayed tool install at a fab is enormous compared to airfreight rates.

Air cargo is already seeing demand pressure from AI infrastructure buildout, and advanced semiconductor manufacturing will intensify that. The Micron investment, combined with Apple and Broadcom's commitments, is essentially a long-duration demand signal for air cargo serving U.S. manufacturing facilities, particularly in upstate New York and Texas.

My read on this

I'm not suggesting reshoring is a mistake, or that these investments won't generate real returns. The Micron bet on U.S. DRAM capacity is strategically coherent given where geopolitics around semiconductor supply are heading, something the Economist noted this week [10] in its coverage of China's own racing semiconductor ambitions. The Toyota move makes sense in a tariff environment where cross-border production carries growing cost and regulatory risk.

What I think is underweighted is the cumulative pressure these investments will place on domestic freight infrastructure, at exactly the moment when that infrastructure is already constrained. Transportation capacity is near-record low. Trucking assets are being consolidated by investors expecting sustained pricing power. Ocean container bookings are elevated. Air cargo is tightening on AI and chip demand.

Reshoring doesn't just require factories. It requires the freight and logistics networks to feed them, staff them, and move what they make. Those networks are not infinitely elastic, and right now they're being asked to absorb both a tariff-driven import surge and the early stages of a decade-long domestic manufacturing buildout at the same time.

The companies that will navigate this best are the ones building freight and logistics capacity into their reshoring models now, not as an afterthought once the factories are running. The ones treating freight as a commodity that will somehow accommodate whatever demand appears are in for an expensive correction.

I think the bill for the reshoring wave is starting to arrive. The freight market is writing the first invoice.
