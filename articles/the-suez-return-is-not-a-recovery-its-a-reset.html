The Suez Return Is Not a Recovery — It's a Reset

Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd just announced the first Gemini service will return to the Suez Canal route. Most people will read this as good news. I think it's more complicated than that.

ocean freightred seasuez canalshipping ratessupply chain resilience

Maersk announced this week that one of its Gemini services, the AE15, will return to the Suez Canal route instead of continuing around the Cape of Good Hope [1]. The first sailing will be the Majestic Maersk. This is a joint decision with Hapag-Lloyd, and the carriers framed it carefully — "a step towards a gradual return," not a full reversal.

Most coverage will treat this as a positive signal. Rates stabilizing, routes normalizing, the Iran conflict ceasefire pact from June starting to take effect [2]. A return to the way things were.

I don't read it that way.

What's actually happening is more interesting and more consequential for anyone making supply chain decisions over the next twelve months. The Suez return is not a recovery to a pre-disruption baseline. It's the beginning of a period where two route systems will coexist, where rate structures will be in flux, and where the gap between shippers who locked in contracts and those who didn't will become very visible.

What the rate data actually shows

The Loadstar reported this week that the pricing spread between Asia-North Europe and Asia-Mediterranean container services has widened to near-record levels [3]. The arbitrage between those two legs has almost never been this large. That's not a normalization signal — that's a market that has been seriously distorted and is trying to find equilibrium while the underlying conditions (Strait of Hormuz congestion, Jeddah port delays running 4-6 hours, uncertain Red Sea security) haven't fully resolved.

FreightWaves noted that a full recovery in ocean shipping capacity and leveling out of rates isn't expected until at least September 2026, even with the ceasefire in place [2]. There's a physical reason for that. Ships that rerouted around the Cape of Good Hope are not instantly redeployable. Vessel positioning takes weeks to work through. Port congestion at key hubs doesn't disappear because a peace deal was signed. And Jeddah, one of the most critical transhipment points for Asia-Europe cargo, is still running delays as of this week [1].

So we have a situation where one major carrier is starting to move one service back through the canal, while the broader capacity picture remains constrained, and spot rates on certain lanes are still near peak levels. That combination is not "recovery." It's a transition period with a lot of variability baked in.

Why this matters for contracts signed right now

Supply Chain Dive reported this week that Lovesac leveraged cargo partnerships to secure freight capacity at contractual rates specifically as a shield against spot market volatility from the Iran war [4]. Burlington did the same. Bob's Discount Furniture is in conversations with carriers about mitigation plans.

These are all furniture and home goods retailers, which tells you something about which sectors felt the Iran conflict most acutely. They tend to run on high-volume ocean freight from Asian manufacturers, with limited ability to absorb sudden rate spikes in already thin-margin businesses.

The contract strategy these companies pursued is now going to face an interesting test. They locked in rates when ocean freight was expensive and uncertain. If the Suez return accelerates, if Gemini's AE15 is followed by more services returning to the canal, spot rates could fall meaningfully by Q4. The companies that locked in contracts for the next year at what looked like "favorable rates" in June [4] may find themselves paying above-market by October.

That's not a criticism of their decision. Securing contracts when you face uncertainty is rational. But it does illustrate the timing problem at the heart of every contracting cycle in freight: you're always pricing a future state based on current conditions, and the current conditions are the most volatile they've been in years.

The compliance problem nobody's talking about

Global Trade Magazine published a piece this week on what it called the hidden export compliance crisis inside the tariff optimization rush [5]. The argument is straightforward: every supply chain team in America is right now reconfiguring routes, diversifying sourcing, rerouting shipments. CFOs and operations directors are all running the same play. And in the rush to move fast, compliance is getting outpaced.

This connects directly to the Suez situation. When carriers start offering Suez-route services again, shippers will face decisions about which goods can move through which lanes under current sanctions regimes, what documentation requirements apply to cargo transiting the Red Sea, and how to manage the compliance exposure of transiting waters that were, until very recently, considered operationally hostile. These aren't theoretical concerns. They're operational questions that procurement and logistics teams will have to answer quickly, under pressure, without necessarily having updated their compliance frameworks.

McCormick received a $28 million tariff refund this week tied to Iran war-related costs [6]. That's a meaningful sum for a company managing logistics and material costs under a lot of pressure. But tariff refunds are retrospective. They don't help you make the right decision at the time. The companies that will navigate this transition best are the ones who have already built the compliance infrastructure to move quickly when conditions change.

The carrier consolidation angle

CMA CGM is acquiring FedEx Supply Chain for $1.4 billion [7]. Once the deal closes, the two companies plan to solidify multiyear ocean and air freight agreements. This is one of the most consequential structural moves in North American logistics this year, and it's happening at exactly the moment when the ocean freight market is in transition.

What CMA CGM is building is an integrated carrier-to-warehouse capability. Ocean freight, domestic logistics, warehouse operations. One entity with end-to-end visibility and pricing power across the chain. For shippers, that creates real leverage concentration. CMA CGM will know more about your supply chain than any single partner you've ever had, and it will be able to price that knowledge into the services it offers.

I think this matters more than the Suez reopening in terms of long-term structural implications. A route normalizing is a temporary condition. A carrier acquiring your 3PL is a permanent change in power dynamics.

The 37th State of Logistics report framed this year's environment as "stronger through disruption," with continuous disruption now a permanent feature of global supply chains [8]. That framing is accurate, but it somewhat obscures what's happening underneath. Disruption doesn't affect all players equally. Large integrated carriers with asset ownership across modes are using this disruption period to consolidate positions that would have taken a decade to build in stable conditions. Smaller shippers and mid-market 3PLs are being squeezed between rate volatility and carrier consolidation simultaneously.

What a "gradual return" actually means operationally

Maersk's language is important here. "Gradual return" and "step towards" are deliberate hedges. The security situation in the Red Sea has not been formally declared safe. The ceasefire with Iran is recent and untested. Jeddah is still congested. The carriers are testing the route with one service before committing the broader fleet.

That means we're entering a period of dual routing, where some services will run Suez and some will continue Cape of Good Hope, potentially for months. The practical consequence for shippers is transit time uncertainty. If you're booking cargo today, you may not know which route your vessel will take. You may not know exactly when it arrives. For industries running lean inventory strategies, that's uncomfortable.

The 37th State report notes that adaptation has become a core competency, not a crisis response [8]. I agree with that framing but want to add something to it. Adaptation is only a competency if it's structured. Retailers and manufacturers who locked in contracts, built compliance infrastructure, and maintained buffer inventory have genuine optionality right now. They can take advantage of route normalization if it happens, absorb it if it doesn't, and make decisions with some cushion.

The ones running purely reactive playbooks — waiting for rates to normalize, deferring contracting decisions, hoping conditions stabilize — are going to find the next six months harder than necessary.

Where I'd focus attention right now

The Suez return is a real signal worth watching. If more Gemini services follow AE15 back through the canal, and if other alliances start to reposition, we could see a meaningful rate correction on Asia-Europe lanes by September or October. That would change the economics of any ocean contract signed in the last 60 days.

But the more durable question is what happens to supply chain power structures as carriers integrate downstream. CMA CGM plus FedEx Supply Chain is one data point. The Tenney Group projected this week that transportation M&A activity is set for a record-breaking 2027 [9]. That pipeline is building.

Shippers who think of freight as a commodity purchased at market rates are going to wake up in 2027 and find that the market has been reorganized around integrated entities with pricing power they never had before.

The Suez announcement is the visible news. The consolidation underneath it is the structural change worth paying attention to.
