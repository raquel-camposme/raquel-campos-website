Truck Rates Are Up 31% and Nobody Seems to Know What to Do About It

Spot truck rates jumped 31% year-over-year in May. That number deserves more attention than it's getting — because the pressures driving it aren't going away, and they're about to collide with a wave of reshoring that assumes cheap domestic freight.

freighttruckingcapacityonshoringcost volatility

There's a data point buried in the U.S. Bank Freight Payment Index from late June that I keep returning to. Spot truck rates rose 31.29% in May compared to a year earlier [1]. Contract rates were up 9%. Per-mile spot pricing climbed from $1.89 in March to $2.14 in May — a meaningful jump in just eight weeks.

Most of the coverage treated this as a trucking recovery story. Three years of freight recession, capacity finally tightening, rates coming back. And that's true, as far as it goes. But I think there's something bigger underneath it, and it connects in uncomfortable ways to several other things happening at once.

The timing matters

When rates recover in a normal freight cycle, you'd expect volume to be climbing too. That's the usual pattern: demand rises, capacity tightens, rates follow. What U.S. Bank is reporting is different. Costs are rising even with lower volumes [1]. Capacity is tightening for structural reasons — carriers that exited during the downturn haven't returned, equipment hasn't been replaced, and the driver pool has continued to age and shrink.

So this isn't a demand spike. It's a supply contraction. And that's a different kind of problem, because you can't solve it by waiting for the cycle to turn.

Now layer in what's happening on the manufacturing side.

Lovesac announced this week that it's starting domestic production of its Sactionals line this summer, as part of a broader tariff-response strategy [2]. They're not alone. The tariff environment has pushed a meaningful number of consumer goods companies toward some version of US manufacturing, whether owned or contracted. The logic is sound: reduce exposure to import duties, shorten freight cycles, improve fulfillment speed.

But there's an assumption baked into all of it that I haven't seen challenged directly. The assumption is that domestic logistics will be cheaper, or at least more stable, than international freight.

That assumption is getting expensive

International ocean freight has its own volatility — the Hormuz situation this quarter has been genuinely disruptive to crude flows and energy pricing [3], which feeds into everything. But over the past few years, it's been possible to argue that ocean shipping was the wild variable, and domestic trucking was the stable baseline.

That's no longer clearly true.

When you reshore production, you're substituting a long ocean leg for a domestic trucking network. That sounds like simplification. In practice, it means your cost structure is now exposed to spot trucking markets, regional capacity constraints, driver availability in specific corridors, and fuel prices that track the same energy volatility that's been making ocean freight unpredictable. You haven't eliminated the variable. You've traded one variable for a different set of them.

And if you're manufacturing in, say, the middle of the country — which is where a lot of greenfield US manufacturing is going, because of land cost and labor availability — you're often moving product across long inland distances before it gets to population centers. That's a lot of truck miles. At $2.14 per mile and rising.

This isn't an argument against reshoring. I want to be clear about that. There are real strategic reasons to diversify away from Asia, and the tariff environment has made the financial case more compelling for certain categories. But operational planning that treats domestic logistics as a solved problem is going to get a rude wake-up call.

The freight forwarder problem

There's one more data point worth pulling in here. Only 13% of freight forwarders and customs brokers rate their own decision-making as excellent [4]. That's from coverage in Logistics Management this week. I'll admit I initially skimmed past it — sounds like the kind of self-assessment statistic that makes the rounds without meaning much.

But I think it actually points at something real. The complexity of current trade conditions — tariff ladders, shifting sourcing geographies, sanctions exposure, carrier consolidation, the ongoing normalization question after the US-Iran ceasefire [4] — is genuinely beyond what most logistics decision-support tools were designed to handle. Freight forwarders are operating in an environment where the rules keep changing and the data is always slightly stale. Of course only 13% feel like they're making excellent decisions. The information conditions don't support excellent decisions.

What that means operationally is that shippers can't assume their 3PL or forwarding partners are seeing around corners for them. The information asymmetry is real, and it goes in the wrong direction: the people with the most to lose from bad routing and timing decisions are the shippers, but the people with the best real-time market access are often the carriers and brokers. TMS platforms are getting more capable — Trimble's new shipper-facing product, built from the Transporeon acquisition, is interesting precisely because it tries to close that gap [5] — but most mid-sized shippers are still flying with limited visibility into what the market is actually doing until they're already in a bad position.

What actually needs to happen

I think there are three things that get underweighted in the current conversation about freight costs.

First, the reshoring wave needs honest landed-cost modeling that includes domestic freight at current rates, not at historical averages or theoretical ideals. A product that was marginally viable to manufacture in the US at $1.89 per spot mile looks different at $2.14 and trending higher. Some companies are going to discover this mid-ramp, which is the worst time to discover anything.

Second, capacity investment in domestic trucking is genuinely urgent, and it's not being treated that way. Driver recruitment and retention, equipment financing, infrastructure for longer-haul corridors — these have long lead times. If manufacturing onshoring continues at pace and trucking capacity doesn't follow, the constraint will show up in fulfillment speed and cost at exactly the moment when companies have committed to their domestic production strategies.

Third, the integration between TMS capability and sourcing decisions is still too weak. Sourcing decisions get made by procurement, freight decisions get made by logistics, and the two models rarely talk to each other in real time. If spot rates for a specific corridor spike, does that feed back into decisions about which DC to ship from, which production sequence to prioritize, which customer promise to protect? In most organizations, not quickly enough. The tools exist to connect these things. The organizational design usually doesn't.

The connection I keep watching

There's a line running through this week's news that hasn't been drawn explicitly anywhere. The US-Iran ceasefire is expected to ease energy prices over time, but logistics normalization will take months [4]. Truck rates are already elevated and rising on structural grounds. Reshoring is adding domestic freight demand. Freight forwarder confidence in their own decisions is low. And TMS adoption for shippers is finally maturing, just as the market complexity justifies it.

None of these things caused the others. But they're all pulling on the same operational thread: the cost and reliability of moving goods inside the United States is becoming a strategic variable in a way it hasn't been for a long time. Companies that treat it as a background assumption are going to be surprised. Companies that treat it as something to actively manage, with real data and integrated decision-making, are going to have an advantage.

Freight is boring until it's not. Right now, it's not.
