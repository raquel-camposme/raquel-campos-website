The North American Rail Map Just Changed. Nobody's Talking About What That Means for Supply Chains.

A rail settlement between CN and Union Pacific quietly reshapes freight connectivity across North America, just as 50% tariffs on Canada and USMCA renegotiations rewrite the rules for cross-border trade. These two things are not unrelated.

railnorth americatariffslogisticsnearshoring

I want to start with something that got almost no attention this week relative to what it probably deserves.

Canadian National announced it will not oppose the Union Pacific-Norfolk Southern merger, and in exchange it gets operating rights over UP's network between Memphis and the Eagle Pass, Texas gateway into Mexico [1]. Union Pacific, for its part, gains access to CN's former Elgin, Joliet and Eastern corridor, a congestion-free route around Chicago that connects to Norfolk Southern's Chicago Line heading east. The deal also unlocks better access to the U.S. Midwest for CN.

Trains.com described CN CEO Tracy Robinson's statement as one of expanding CN's "north-south franchise" and creating "new routes for customers" between Canada and Mexico [1]. That framing is accurate but understated. What actually happened here is that a major new freight artery across North America just got drawn, at the exact moment when the political geography of North American trade is being violently redrawn by tariff policy.

That timing matters.

What the Rail Deal Actually Creates

Think about the network implications for a moment. Union Pacific will now have a clean shot from the West Coast, around Chicago without the usual congestion, and onward to Norfolk Southern's eastern network. That is a near coast-to-coast single-carrier corridor with dramatically reduced interchange friction.

CN, meanwhile, extends its reach from Canada's major ports all the way south to Eagle Pass, which is one of the busiest land border crossings into Mexico. This gives CN something it never had before: genuine single-line competition with CPKC, the Canadian Pacific Kansas City railroad that has been marketing its Canada-to-Mexico capability as a differentiator since its own merger completed in 2023.

CPKC spent years building the argument that it was the only railroad that could move freight seamlessly from Canada through the U.S. to Mexico on one ticket. That argument just got significantly harder to sustain. CN can now make a similar claim, with different geography and different terminal access.

For shippers moving goods north-south through the continent, the competitive landscape for rail just changed in ways that will take months to fully price out. And for companies actively rerouting supply chains because of tariff pressure, this matters immediately.

The Canada Tariff Problem Is Not Small

On July 20, the Trump administration announced 50% tariffs on certain Canadian imports, with USMCA renegotiations with Mexico resuming at the same time [2]. These are not symmetrical events, even though they arrived in the same week.

Canada occupies a different position in U.S. supply chains than Mexico does. Canada is a major supplier of industrial inputs: steel, aluminum, lumber, auto parts, energy. A 50% tariff on Canadian goods doesn't just make finished Canadian products more expensive. It cascades through American manufacturing that has been quietly sourcing from Canadian producers for decades under the assumption that the border was essentially frictionless.

The automotive sector is the obvious example. Vehicle production along the Detroit-Windsor corridor has always treated the Canada-U.S. border as more of an administrative line than a real supply chain barrier. Parts cross that border multiple times during assembly. A 50% tariff doesn't just add cost once. Depending on how tariffs are assessed on intermediate goods that cross back and forth, the cost accumulation can be severe. I've seen estimates that auto parts in that corridor can cross the border six or seven times before a vehicle is complete.

Lumber and construction materials are a different pressure point. U.S. homebuilders are already squeezed, and Canadian lumber has historically been a meaningful portion of the domestic supply. A 50% tariff on top of already elevated housing costs is going to filter through quickly.

Freight volumes between Canada and the U.S. will respond to this. Not all at once, but directionally, some of what moved north-south by truck and rail will either shift to domestically sourced alternatives (where they exist) or get repriced substantially. That affects carriers, 3PLs, and any manufacturer who built a cross-border supply relationship under the old cost assumptions.

Two Things Happening at Once

Here is what I find genuinely interesting about this week's news. The rail deal and the Canada tariffs are happening simultaneously, and they're pointing in somewhat different directions.

The tariffs are disrupting existing north-south flows. The rail deal is building infrastructure for new north-south flows. The CN-UP settlement isn't specifically a response to tariff policy, of course. These deals take years to negotiate. But the timing creates an odd situation where a major freight connectivity upgrade is landing just as the commercial logic for north-south trade is being stress-tested.

For Mexico, the picture is somewhat more favorable. USMCA talks with Mexico are resuming [2], and while nothing is settled, the political appetite to find a workable framework with Mexico appears greater than with Canada right now. And CN's new access to Eagle Pass directly benefits Mexico-bound and Mexico-originating traffic. If nearshoring to Mexico continues, and most signals suggest it will despite the uncertainty, better rail access from Mexico through the U.S. to Canada or to eastern ports is an asset.

The Latin American dimension adds another layer. Brazil is now under a 25% tariff rate for U.S. trade [3], while the EU-Mercosur agreement is pushing South American exporters toward European markets. BYD just rolled its 100,000th vehicle off its Brazilian assembly line and already has orders for Argentina and Mexico [4]. What BYD is building in Brazil isn't just a manufacturing base for South American consumption. Camacari is positioned as a regional export hub. If Mexican tariff treatment stabilizes under USMCA, and CN has better rail reach into Mexico, then Brazilian-built vehicles exported to Mexico and moving into North American distribution networks via improved rail corridors becomes a real logistics question, not a theoretical one.

The Pharma Tariff Wrinkle

Elsewhere in this week's news, the U.S. announced phased tariffs on imported generic drugs: 100% by August 2028, rising to 200% by 2029 [5]. The stated intention is reshoring pharmaceutical production. The mechanism is blunt.

Generic drug supply chains are genuinely among the most globalized in the world, as FreightWaves noted [5]. Active pharmaceutical ingredients are largely manufactured in India and China. Finished generics are produced in India, Eastern Europe, Canada, and elsewhere. The three major U.S. drug distributors, which control most of the domestic pharmaceutical distribution network, depend on this structure.

The phased timeline gives manufacturers two years before the first tariff kicks in. That sounds like a reasonable window until you consider what "building a pharmaceutical plant in the United States" actually involves. Site selection, permitting, equipment procurement, regulatory approval from the FDA, and workforce hiring for highly specialized production are not a two-year process. Most industry estimates suggest five to seven years is realistic for a greenfield pharmaceutical manufacturing facility to reach full operational capacity.

So the tariff will arrive before the reshoring can realistically happen. What fills the gap is higher prices, substitution effects, and possible shortages for lower-margin generics where the economics of U.S. production never pencil out regardless of the tariff level.

The supply chain implication is that distributors and hospital systems will need to start stress-testing their supplier lists now, well ahead of 2028, because the secondary effects on sourcing decisions and inventory buffering will show up long before the tariffs take effect.

Why Rail Keeps Getting Underrated

I want to return to the CN-UP settlement because I think it represents something structurally important that tends to get undercovered relative to ocean shipping and air freight.

Rail is the connective tissue for bulk industrial goods in North America: auto parts, agricultural commodities, chemicals, building materials, intermodal containers from port to inland distribution. When the railroad map changes, the competitive geography for logistics changes with it. Shippers who locked in agreements or network designs based on the old map will need to revisit them.

The congestion-free Chicago bypass that UP now gains access to through the former EJ&E is not a minor detail. Chicago is the single largest rail interchange point in North America. Almost every major east-west rail move touches it. The fact that UP will now have a way around the bottleneck, with direct access to Norfolk Southern's eastern network, changes the economics of intermodal moves for dozens of major shippers.

CN's access to Eagle Pass, meanwhile, makes it a genuine player in the U.S.-Mexico intermodal market for the first time. That is a competitive shift that CPKC will feel.

Taken together with the tariff environment reshaping where goods are sourced and made, I think this week's rail news is significantly more important for supply chain planners than it appeared in the headlines. The network is changing. The tariff environment is changing. Both of them are changing at the same time. That combination creates real decisions for anyone managing cross-border flows in North America, and the window for updating your logistics assumptions is shorter than it might feel.
